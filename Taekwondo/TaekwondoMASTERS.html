Choi Hong Hi

Choi Hong Hi (1918–2002) was a Korean military general and martial artist who is widely recognized as one of the founders of modern Taekwondo. Often called the “Father of Taekwondo,” he played a major role in developing, organizing, and spreading the martial art across the world. He was not only a taekwondo master but also a military leader, educator, and promoter of Korean culture. Choi Hong Hi was born on November 9, 1918, in Hwa Dae, Korea, during the period when Korea was under Japanese occupation. As a child, he was physically weak and often sick, which led his parents to encourage him to learn martial arts to improve his strength and discipline. He studied calligraphy under a teacher who also secretly taught him Taekkyeon, a traditional Korean martial art known for its kicking techniques.

Later, while studying in Japan, Choi learned Karate and eventually earned a black belt. These early experiences strongly influenced the martial art he would later help create. After Korea was liberated from Japanese rule in 1945, Choi joined the South Korean military and quickly rose through the ranks, eventually becoming a general. During his military service, he trained soldiers in hand-to-hand combat and began combining elements of Korean martial arts with karate techniques. In the 1950s, Choi and other martial artists worked to develop a uniquely Korean martial art. On April 11, 1955, the name “Taekwondo” was officially adopted. The word combines three meanings: “Tae” meaning foot or kicking, “Kwon” meaning fist or punching, and “Do” meaning way or discipline. Together, Taekwondo means “the way of the foot and fist.” Choi believed Taekwondo was much more than a fighting system. He saw it as a way to improve character, self-control, confidence, and moral values. He emphasized important principles such as courtesy, integrity, perseverance, self-control, and indomitable spirit. According to him, martial arts training should help people grow mentally as well as physically. In 1966, Choi founded the International Taekwon-Do Federation (ITF), which played an important role in spreading Taekwondo around the world. He traveled to many countries, teaching and promoting the martial art throughout Asia, Europe, the Americas, and the Middle East. Because of his efforts, Taekwondo became one of the most popular martial arts worldwide and is now practiced by millions of people for self-defense, fitness, discipline, and competition. However, Choi’s life also involved political controversy. Due to disagreements with the South Korean government, he left South Korea in the 1970s and later settled in Canada. He continued promoting ITF Taekwondo internationally and cooperated with North Korea in spreading the martial art, which caused criticism because of political tensions between North and South Korea. Despite this, Choi remained committed to teaching and developing Taekwondo. Throughout his life, Choi developed training patterns known as tuls and wrote books explaining Taekwondo techniques, philosophy, and teaching methods. His work helped establish a clear structure for practitioners and instructors around the world. Choi Hong Hi died on June 15, 2002, in Pyongyang at the age of 83. Even after his death, his influence remains strong, especially in ITF Taekwondo schools that continue to teach his methods and philosophy. Today, he is remembered as one of the most important figures in martial arts history because he transformed Taekwondo into a global discipline that teaches not only physical skill but also respect, discipline, and strong moral character.

Rhee Ki-ha

Rhee Ki-ha is a South Korean Taekwondo master and one of the most important pioneers of Taekwondo in Europe. Born on March 20, 1938, in South Korea, he became one of the earliest students of General Choi Hong Hi, the founder of modern Taekwondo. Rhee Ki-ha is widely known as the “Father of British Taekwondo” because of his major role in introducing and developing Taekwondo in the United Kingdom. He achieved the rank of Grandmaster and became highly respected for his teaching, discipline, and dedication to spreading the philosophy of Taekwondo around the world.

In the 1960s, Rhee Ki-ha was sent by Choi Hong Hi to the United Kingdom to introduce Taekwondo, becoming one of the first Korean instructors to teach the martial art in Europe. At first, Taekwondo was not widely known in Britain, so Rhee faced many challenges in attracting students and establishing schools. Despite this, he worked tirelessly to teach classes, demonstrate techniques, and explain the values behind Taekwondo, such as discipline, respect, perseverance, and self-control. Over time, his efforts helped Taekwondo grow in popularity across the UK and Europe. He played an important role in the development of the International Taekwon-Do Federation (ITF), helping organize seminars, competitions, and instructor training. Through decades of teaching, Rhee influenced thousands of students, many of whom later became instructors themselves and spread Taekwondo even further. He became known not only for his technical skill but also for his strong character and loyalty to the teachings of Choi Hong Hi. His contribution helped transform Taekwondo from a relatively unknown Korean martial art into an internationally respected discipline practiced by people of all ages. Today, Rhee Ki-ha is remembered as one of the most influential Taekwondo masters in history, especially for his role in shaping British and European Taekwondo and inspiring generations of practitioners.

Jhoon Rhee

Jhoon Rhee was a Korean-American Taekwondo master, martial artist, and instructor who is widely known as the “Father of American Taekwondo.” Born on January 7, 1932, in South Korea, he became one of the earliest martial artists to introduce and popularize Taekwondo in the United States. Jhoon Rhee began training in martial arts at a young age and later studied under important Korean masters during the early development of modern Taekwondo. He eventually moved to the United States to continue his education and teach martial arts, becoming one of the most respected Taekwondo instructors in American history. He was known not only for his fighting skill and teaching ability but also for promoting discipline, respect, self-confidence, and peace through martial arts.

In the 1950s and 1960s, Jhoon Rhee began opening martial arts schools in the United States at a time when Taekwondo was still largely unknown to Americans. He faced challenges in introducing a foreign martial art but worked hard to attract students through demonstrations, teaching programs, and public performances. His schools became very successful, and he trained thousands of students, including military personnel, celebrities, politicians, and ordinary families. Jhoon Rhee is also credited with helping modernize martial arts training by introducing safer equipment, such as foam protective gear, which made sparring more accessible and reduced injuries. He became famous for his philosophy of self-discipline and positive thinking, often encouraging students to build strong character alongside physical skill. Through television appearances, demonstrations, and years of teaching, he helped make Taekwondo one of the most popular martial arts in the United States. His influence spread far beyond martial arts schools, as many of his students later became instructors and continued teaching his methods and values. Jhoon Rhee passed away on April 30, 2018, but his legacy remains strong, and he is remembered as one of the most influential figures in martial arts history for bringing Taekwondo into mainstream American culture and inspiring generations of practitioners.

Park Jong-soo

Park Jong-soo is a South Korean Taekwondo master and one of the most respected pioneers in the history of International Taekwondo. He is widely recognized for his important role in helping spread Taekwondo around the world and for preserving the traditional teachings of the art. Park Jong-soo became one of the early students of General Choi Hong Hi, who is considered the founder of modern Taekwondo. Through years of training, dedication, and discipline, Park developed into one of the highest-ranking and most skilled Taekwondo masters of his generation. He became well known for his strong technical ability, leadership, and commitment to teaching Taekwondo not only as a fighting system but also as a way of improving discipline, respect, confidence, and moral character. Over the years, he earned the rank of Grandmaster and became one of the most influential figures in the development of International Taekwondo, especially within the ITF tradition.

During the early years of Taekwondo’s development, Park Jong-soo played a major role in helping introduce the martial art to countries outside Korea. As part of international demonstration teams led by Choi Hong Hi, he traveled extensively across Europe, Asia, the Americas, and other regions to promote Taekwondo and teach new students. At that time, Taekwondo was still relatively unknown outside Korea, so Park and other instructors faced the challenge of explaining and demonstrating the effectiveness and philosophy of the martial art to unfamiliar audiences. Through demonstrations, seminars, competitions, and instructor courses, he helped establish Taekwondo schools and inspired many people to begin training. Park became known for emphasizing precise techniques, discipline, self-control, perseverance, and respect for others, believing that Taekwondo should strengthen both the body and the mind. He was also deeply involved in the work of the International Taekwon-Do Federation (ITF), helping maintain teaching standards and contributing to the worldwide growth of the organization. Throughout his long career, he trained thousands of practitioners and instructors, many of whom later opened their own schools and continued spreading his knowledge. His dedication to preserving traditional ITF techniques and the teachings of Choi Hong Hi earned him great respect in the martial arts community. Even today, Park Jong-soo is remembered as one of the most influential Taekwondo masters in history because of his lifelong contribution to teaching, promoting, and preserving Taekwondo as both a martial art and a philosophy for personal growth.

Kong Young-il

Kong Young-il is a highly respected Taekwondo master and an important figure in the development and promotion of International Taekwondo. Born in Korea, he became known for his dedication to teaching, technical skill, and contribution to spreading Taekwondo internationally. Kong Young-il trained under the influence of the ITF system developed by General Choi Hong Hi and became recognized as one of the senior masters responsible for preserving traditional Taekwondo techniques and philosophy. Over many years of training and teaching, he achieved a high rank and earned respect among practitioners for his discipline, leadership, and deep understanding of the martial art. He became known not only as an instructor but also as an ambassador of Taekwondo, helping represent the art at seminars, demonstrations, and international events.

During his career, Kong Young-il played an important role in promoting Taekwondo through teaching programs, international seminars, and instructor development. He worked to spread the principles of Taekwondo, including courtesy, integrity, perseverance, self-control, and indomitable spirit, believing that martial arts training should improve both physical skill and personal character. Through his involvement in the International Taekwon-Do Federation (ITF), he helped support the growth of Taekwondo around the world and influenced many students and instructors who later continued teaching future generations. Kong Young-il became respected for his precise technical knowledge and commitment to maintaining traditional methods of instruction established during the early years of Taekwondo. His work contributed to strengthening the international reputation of Taekwondo as both a competitive martial art and a system of personal development. Today, he is remembered as one of the important senior masters of ITF Taekwondo whose dedication and influence helped preserve and expand the art internationally, inspiring practitioners through his teaching, discipline, and commitment to the values of Taekwondo.

Choi Kwang-jo

Choi Kwang-jo is a South Korean martial artist best known as the founder of Choi Kwang-Do, a modern martial art system based on scientific movement and natural body mechanics. He originally trained in traditional Taekwondo and became a highly skilled practitioner and instructor before developing his own approach to martial arts. His system was designed to reduce injuries while improving efficiency, flexibility, and overall health, making it accessible to people of all ages and physical abilities.

Choi Kwang-jo began his martial arts journey in traditional Taekwondo, where he trained intensively and became a respected instructor and competitor. However, over time he became concerned about the high rate of injuries in many traditional martial arts due to rigid techniques and repeated impact training. This led him to study human anatomy, biomechanics, and movement science in order to redesign martial arts techniques in a safer and more natural way. In 1987, he officially founded Choi Kwang-Do, introducing a new system that focused on fluid motion, relaxation, breathing, and scientifically efficient movement rather than brute force or extreme impact. His approach emphasized health benefits such as stress reduction, rehabilitation, improved posture, and long-term physical well-being, making it different from many traditional combat-focused martial arts. Choi Kwang-jo expanded his system internationally, opening schools and training instructors in many countries. His teaching method attracted not only martial artists but also people seeking fitness, therapy, and personal development. One of his main goals was to make martial arts safe for children, older adults, and people recovering from injuries, while still preserving self-defense effectiveness. He often emphasized that martial arts should support lifelong health rather than cause long-term damage, which made his system unique in the martial arts world. His ideas sometimes created debate among traditional martial artists, especially those who preferred more combat-oriented training, but his influence continued to grow steadily. Over time, Choi Kwang-jo became recognized as an innovator who challenged traditional thinking and introduced a more scientific and health-focused approach to martial arts practice. Today, he is remembered as an important figure who expanded the understanding of martial arts beyond combat, showing that they can also be a path to wellness, rehabilitation, and balanced living.

Nguyen Van Binh

Nguyen Van Binh is a Vietnamese Taekwondo practitioner and instructor known for his role in developing and supporting Taekwondo training within Vietnam’s martial arts community. He is associated with the spread of Korean Taekwondo in Southeast Asia, where the martial art became increasingly popular as both a sport and a system of discipline and personal development. Like many instructors of his generation, he contributed to building dojangs (training halls), guiding students through structured training, and helping establish Taekwondo as a recognized martial art in local communities. His work reflects the broader international expansion of Taekwondo created by pioneers such as Choi Hong Hi, whose system inspired instructors worldwide to adapt and teach the art in different cultural settings.

Over time, Nguyen Van Binh’s role is understood mainly through his contribution as a teacher, mentor, and supporter of Taekwondo development at a regional level. In countries like Vietnam, Taekwondo grew rapidly thanks to dedicated instructors who focused on consistent training, discipline, and youth development programs. He helped train students not only in kicking, striking, and self-defense techniques but also in the philosophical side of Taekwondo, such as respect, humility, perseverance, and self-control. Many of his students would have continued training and possibly become instructors themselves, creating a chain of knowledge transfer that is essential for martial arts growth. His work would have involved organizing classes, guiding beginners through basic forms (poomsae), and helping advanced students improve sparring skills and technical precision. Even though he is not widely documented in international martial arts history compared to global figures, his importance lies in the local impact of his teaching, where Taekwondo becomes part of everyday life, physical education, and youth development. His contribution also fits into the larger story of how Taekwondo evolved from a Korean martial art into a global system practiced in schools, universities, military programs, and sports competitions. Instructors like Nguyen Van Binh helped bridge the gap between traditional Korean masters and local students by adapting teaching styles to different languages, cultures, and learning environments. This process required patience, communication skills, and strong personal discipline, as instructors often had to introduce new training methods and explain unfamiliar concepts to beginners. Through regular practice and structured progression, students under his guidance would have developed physical strength, flexibility, coordination, and mental focus. More importantly, Taekwondo training under instructors like him also aimed to build character, encouraging students to apply martial arts values in everyday life such as respect for teachers, self-control in conflict situations, and determination in facing challenges. Even if not internationally famous, his role represents the essential foundation of Taekwondo’s global success: thousands of dedicated instructors who ensured the martial art continued to grow steadily across different countries and generations.

Charles E. Sereff

Charles E. Sereff is an American Taekwondo Grandmaster and one of the most important pioneers in the development and spread of International Taekwondo in the United States. He is best known as the founder of Sereff Taekwon-Do, one of the largest and most influential organizations practicing the ITF (International Taekwon-Do Federation) style in North America. As a direct student of General Choi Hong Hi, he was part of the early generation of non-Korean masters who trained closely under the founder of Taekwondo himself, which gave him a deep understanding of the original system, its philosophy, and its technical structure. Through this connection, Sereff became a major figure responsible for introducing authentic ITF Taekwondo to Western practitioners and ensuring that the traditional methods, patterns (tuls), and training standards were preserved and taught correctly in a new cultural environment where Taekwondo was still developing and growing in popularity.

Over the course of his long martial arts career, Charles E. Sereff devoted his life to teaching, organizing, and expanding Taekwondo across the United States and internationally, building one of the strongest ITF communities outside of Korea. After years of direct training under Choi Hong Hi, he became deeply involved in instructor development, certification programs, and the technical standardization of Taekwondo practice, ensuring that students and instructors maintained a consistent and high level of skill. He founded the Sereff Taekwon-Do Association, which grew into a large network of schools producing thousands of black belts and competitive athletes who participated in national and international events. His teaching style emphasized discipline, precision, respect, and strong fundamentals, while also maintaining a deep focus on the philosophical side of Taekwondo, including self-control, perseverance, humility, and indomitable spirit. He played a major role in organizing seminars, training camps, and international gatherings that helped unify and strengthen the ITF community in North America, bringing instructors together and raising overall technical standards. Over time, many of his students became instructors themselves, spreading his influence even further across generations. Today, Charles E. Sereff is remembered as one of the most significant figures in the global expansion of ITF Taekwondo, and his legacy continues through the thousands of practitioners and instructors who still follow the traditional system he helped establish and preserve.

He is recognized as one of the senior masters who helped preserve, teach, and spread traditional ITF Taekwondo techniques and philosophy. Over many decades, Hwang Kwang-sung became respected for his technical knowledge, discipline, and dedication to maintaining the original teachings of Taekwondo as both a martial art and a way of personal development.

Sun Hwan Chung

Sun Hwan Chung is a South Korean Taekwondo Grandmaster known for his role in the development and international spread of traditional ITF Taekwondo. He is widely recognized as one of the senior masters associated with the early generation of students who trained under General Choi Hong Hi, the founder of modern Taekwondo. Through his training and long-term dedication, Sun Hwan Chung became an important figure in preserving the original teachings, techniques, and philosophy of ITF Taekwondo. He earned respect for his deep technical understanding, disciplined approach to training, and commitment to teaching Taekwondo as both a martial art and a system for personal growth and character development.

Over many decades, Sun Hwan Chung contributed to Taekwondo through teaching, instructor development, and international promotion of the ITF system. He helped train students and instructors in correct technique, focusing strongly on precision, balance, timing, and proper execution of patterns (tuls), sparring, and self-defense applications. His teaching emphasized not only physical skill but also the moral and philosophical values of Taekwondo, including respect, humility, perseverance, integrity, and self-control. He was involved in seminars and training programs that helped standardize Taekwondo instruction across different countries, ensuring that students around the world could learn the same traditional system. Through his work, many of his students became instructors themselves, continuing to spread Taekwondo in their own regions and strengthening the global ITF community. Today, Sun Hwan Chung is remembered as one of the important masters who helped maintain the original spirit of Taekwondo while supporting its growth into a worldwide martial art practiced by millions of people.
